Event 1 Report: AWS Learning, Hackathon, Confidence, and Procrastination

Event Objectives

This event gave me a broader view of both technical and personal skills needed in the technology field. The content was not only about learning AWS, but also covered practical topics such as hackathons, product building, confidence, and overcoming procrastination.

Through the sessions, I learned more about visual tools for learning AWS, how a team works under pressure during a hackathon, the role of confidence in communication and interviews, and the deeper reasons behind procrastination. These lessons are useful not only for studying but also for my future work.

Speakers

  • Huynh Thai Linh - Topic: Level Up Your AWS Skills with Cloud Quest and Floci
  • The Baller, including Huynh An Khuong, Mai Quoc Anh, Nguyen Tran Minh Quan - Topic: HACKATHON “HA! a tons” “of fun” “and bug fixes” “and back pains” “and sleep deprivation”
  • Nguyen Thi Quynh Nhu - Topic: Why We Always Need Confidence
  • Nghia Tran - Sharing about a career transition journey and the Tu Vi Dai Viet project
  • Khac Uy Pham - Topic: The Iceberg of Procrastination - When “Laziness” is Just the Tip of Fear

Key Highlights

Level Up Your AWS Skills with Cloud Quest and Floci

Huynh Thai Linh’s session introduced two useful tools for learning and practicing AWS: AWS Cloud Quest and Floci. Both tools help learners approach cloud computing in a more practical way instead of only reading documentation or studying theory.

AWS Cloud Quest is a 3D game-based learning platform for AWS. Learners join a simulated city environment and complete cloud-related missions to develop that city. This makes AWS easier to understand because each mission is connected to a specific real-world scenario. Through Cloud Quest, I could better imagine how service groups such as compute, storage, database, and security are used in practice.

Floci is an open-source tool that simulates an AWS environment on a local machine. It allows developers to practice, develop, and test AWS services without deploying directly to the real cloud. This helps reduce cost and risk, which is especially useful for students and beginners. I found Floci helpful for testing APIs, checking logic, and preparing a system before deploying it to a real environment.

The Baller’s Hackathon Journey

The sharing from The Baller helped me understand the real experience of joining a hackathon. The team talked about how they formed ideas, divided tasks, fixed bugs, worked under time pressure, and completed a product within a short period of time.

What impressed me was that a hackathon does not only require programming skills. It also requires teamwork, communication, time management, and the ability to adapt quickly. During product development, errors, lack of sleep, and repeated fixes are almost unavoidable. However, these pressures help participants develop problem-solving skills and learn how to finish a product in a realistic environment.

From this session, I realized that hackathons are a good environment for students to gain experience. Besides learning technical skills, participants can also expand their network, learn from capable peers, and prepare better for future career opportunities.

Why We Always Need Confidence

Nguyen Thi Quynh Nhu’s session focused on the role of confidence in learning, communication, and career development. Confidence helps people present ideas more clearly, communicate more effectively in teams, perform better in interviews, and take on new opportunities.

On the other hand, lack of confidence can make students afraid to speak up, avoid sharing opinions, underestimate themselves, and miss important opportunities. I found this topic close to my own experience because in school or work, without enough confidence, it is easy to miss chances to show my ability.

An important lesson is that confidence should be based on real capability. To become more confident, I need to prepare carefully before presentations or interviews, start with small actions, join more activities, and accept mistakes as part of the learning process.

Career Transition And The Tu Vi Dai Viet Project

Nghia Tran’s sharing gave me another perspective on career transition and building a real product. The Tu Vi Dai Viet project combines technology with traditional astrology knowledge. Users enter personal information, the system generates an astrology chart, and then the system analyzes the data based on different service levels.

The main lesson I learned is that a real product does not only need technical skills. It also requires domain knowledge, data, user needs, and a clear understanding of the value the product brings to users. Developers should not focus only on coding, but also need to understand the problem they are solving.

The Iceberg of Procrastination

Khac Uy Pham’s session analyzed procrastination through the iceberg model. Procrastination is not simply laziness. Under the surface, it may come from fear of failure, lack of confidence, pressure to be perfect, or not knowing where to start.

This topic was practical for me because sometimes I also think too much before starting a task. The key lesson is that I should not wait until everything is perfect before taking action. Instead, I should begin with small steps, reduce pressure, and keep making progress little by little.

What I Learned

About AWS

  • Cloud Quest makes AWS learning more visual, accessible, and less dry.
  • Floci supports AWS practice in a local environment, which is useful for learning, testing, and development.
  • Combining theory with hands-on practice helps me understand cloud services more deeply.

About Hackathons

  • Hackathons help improve problem-solving skills and the ability to work under pressure.
  • Teamwork, communication, and clear task division are important for completing a product.
  • Real experience can teach students many things that theory alone cannot fully provide.

About Confidence

  • Confidence helps me present ideas and show my ability better.
  • Confidence should be built through preparation, practice, and experience.
  • Confidence should be balanced with self-awareness to avoid being overconfident.

About Product Building

  • A good product needs a combination of technology, data, domain knowledge, and user needs.
  • Developers need to understand the real problem before building a solution.
  • Career transition or starting a new field requires persistence and a clear direction.

About Procrastination

  • Procrastination can come from deeper reasons such as fear of mistakes, fear of failure, or lack of direction.
  • Starting with small tasks helps reduce pressure and makes action easier.
  • Taking action early is better than waiting for perfect conditions.

Applying The Lessons To Myself

After the event, I can apply these lessons to my own study and practice. I can use Cloud Quest to learn AWS in a more visual way, use Floci to test AWS services locally, and join activities such as hackathons to gain more real-world experience.

Besides that, I need to improve my confidence by preparing better before presentations, asking questions more actively, and participating in real projects. To reduce procrastination, I should break tasks into smaller parts, start earlier, and build the habit of completing work step by step instead of waiting for perfect conditions.

Event Experience

For me, this event was useful because the content was diverse and combined technical knowledge with personal development skills. The sessions helped me learn more about AWS and also understand the skills needed to grow in the technology industry.

I was especially impressed by the hackathon and procrastination sessions because they are closely related to students’ learning process. The hackathon session showed the importance of practical experience, while the procrastination session helped me reflect on how I manage time and start tasks. Overall, the event motivated me to study more proactively, become more confident, and be more willing to try new opportunities.

Lessons Learned

  • Learning AWS is more effective when theory, visual tools, and hands-on practice are combined.
  • Experiences such as hackathons help improve both technical skills and soft skills.
  • Confidence is important in communication, interviews, and career development.
  • When building a product, it is necessary to care about user needs and domain knowledge.
  • To overcome procrastination, I should start with small steps and keep taking consistent action.

Conclusion

Through Event 1, I gained useful knowledge not only about AWS but also about learning methods, teamwork, product building, and personal development. The event helped me understand that to improve in the technology field, students need to combine technical knowledge, practical experience, and personal skills. I also realized that I need to be more proactive in learning AWS, joining real activities, building confidence, and reducing procrastination. These are important lessons that help me better orient my learning and future work.
